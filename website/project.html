Caustic Design

Caustic is an area of reflected or refracted light, often curve shaped with high illumination. Such caustics are caused by either reflective or transparent objects as glasses, water or metallic objects.
In this project, we utilize the refractive behaviour of acrylic glass in order to create a caustic image. We will thereby take a target image as an input that we will in the end be able to project onto a receiving plane.
We will give an overview over the relevant steps to come from an image to a shaped acrylic glass object and present few results we achieved with our implementation.

Cube made of shower glass produces caustic.

Overview

Figure 1: Algorithm overview. Source: [1]

Figure 1 gives an overview over the algorithm. We start from the left. First we have a light source, that provides incident illumination which passes the acrylic object (caustic generator) and forms the source irradiance on the receiving plane. Thereby, the source surface is the outgoing surface of our acrylic glass object. We then need to find an Optimal Transport Map between the source- and the target-irradiance. Given this Optimal Transport Map, we can calculate where each light-ray needs to be redirected to andon we can calculate what the normals need to be to achieve the desired refraction of the light-rays.
This brings us the final step, the Target Optimization. In this step, we calculate how the source surface needs to be modified to be as close to the desired normals as possible. The resulting surface is called the target surface.

Algorithm

The algorithm comprises of three main parts we will describe here, namely:

  • Optimal Transport Map (OTM) Computation
  • Natural Neighbor Interpolation
  • Target Optimization

Optimal Transport Map Computation

The Optimal Transport Map (OTM) is a mapping between the initial Voronoi diagram and the optimized power diagram. To obtain the OTM, we first create a Voronoi diagram from the source distribution (1). Each cell of the Voronoi diagram thereby contains the same amount of irradiance (same amount of light). This means, that areas with less irradiance are covered by larger cells. To achieve this, we apply Lloyd Sampling.
In the next step, we remove the source distribution an plug in the target distribution. We now have a non-matching Voronoi-diagram-distribution pair (2). At this point, we transform the Voronoi diagram into a power diagram (or Weighted Voronoi diagram) by introducing weights. Each cell has an associated weight. If all weights are zero, the power diagram corresponds to the original Voronoi diagram. By increasing the weight of a cell, this cell grows, by decreasing the weight, the corresponding cell shrinks.
Step (3) finally optimizes the weight vector of the Power diagram to again have the same irradiance in each cell and at the same time, moves each cell as few as possible. This process is a convex optimization, for which we use an L-BFGS solver [6].

Having the Optimal Transport Map, we know where each cell from the initial Voronoi diagram moves to and therefore have a direct mapping from the initial Voronoi diagram to the optimized power diagram.
Figure 2: OTM Steps. Source: [1]


Multi-Scale Approach

As calculating the OTM is a convex optimization, we can plug in an initial guess. The quality of this initial guess has an significant impact on the duration of the optimization process. Therefore, we calculate the OTM on different levels. We thereby start with fewer cells and a lower-resolution target distribution image on the lowest level. We then use the result of that optimization as an initial guess for the next higher level. This step is repeated several times until we reach our target level that we want to compute.

Figure 3: Weight-injection for Multi-Scale-Approach

In Figure 3, one can see the lower level Voronoi diagram on the left and the next higher Voronoi diagram on the right. When the optimization of the lower level diagram succeeds, we use the weight of the highlighted cell and inject it as an initial guess for each cell highlighted in the right diagram.

In the following, we have two videos that show the process of calculating the OTM. On the left, the video shows the creation of the initial Voronoi diagram. On the right, the weights of the previously calculated initial Voronoi diagram (or power diagram, as we are working with weights now) are optimized using a manually implemented Gradient Descent approach. The calculation using the aforementioned L-BFGS solver is faster, but the manual implementation provides a better visualization of the optimization process. One can see, that the cells are moving towards the center of the image. Natural Neighbor Interpolation

At this point, we have an Optimal Transport Map, which tells us, where each cell of the initial Voronoi diagram moves to. But as we want the source surface to be a regular grid, we need to map the source surface vertices to the initial Voronoi diagram. The desired mapping can be achieved by using the Natural Neighbor Interpolation.
The Natural Neighbor Interpolation simulates an additional insertion of a cell into a Voronoi diagram and calculates how much area this new cell would steal from the neighboring cells. This hypothetically stolen area per neighboring cell can be used as a weighting for a linear combination of the positions of the cells' centroid's in the optimized power diagram.
We are applying a Natural Neighbor Interpolation for each vertex of our source surface to determine, where a light-ray that passes a given vertex is supposed to intersect our receiving plane.

As shown in Figure 4, a light-ray passing through a vertex xS in our source mesh (mesh of the source surface) is ray-traced to find the intersection with the receiving plane in the source distribution x̃R. The light-ray direction is hereby shown as dS. We now apply the Natural Neighbor Interpolation to determine how much area the insertion of xR would steal from the neighboring cells. With the given areas (used as weights) we take a linear combination of the positions of the neighboring cells' centroid's in the optimized power diagram to retrieve the desired intersection-point xR.

Figure 4: Finding the target point xR from source point xS. Source: [1]

Target Optimization

The Target Optimization, as given in Figure 5, contains two steps, namely target normal computation and 3D position optimization. Since the 3D position optimization the results of the target normal computation, these two steps iterated several times until convergence is reached. For each vertex x in our mesh, we have a desired point of intersection xR with the receiving plane that we calculated using the Natural Neighbor Interpolation. In the target normal computation, we use this given information to calculate the desired normal direction ñ for the given vertex x. With this desired normal ñ, the ray passing vertex x will be refracted in the direction of dT. For the calculation of ñ, we use the Fresnel Mapping Equation with dI being the incoming light direction:

The optimization of the 3D position is formulated as on optimization process that minimizes four geometric constraints. Each of those constraints are described by an energy with an associated weight in order to adjust the importants of each energy.
Those energies are described in the following.

Geometric Constraints
Definition Description

The integration energy aligns the desired normal ñ with the normal n.

The direction energy ensures that the vertex x is not moving too far away from the initial vertex position xS on the y-z-plane

The regularization energy is minimal if the triangles are well shaped and regular as given in the upper image. If this term is left out, a target surface as given in the lower image might occur.

The barrier energy prevents the vertices to move too far away from the receiving plane.

Fabrication

We use a CNC milling machine to mill acrylic glass. The milling process includes several steps (roughing, z-level, planar finishing) with different tools (4mm/3mm, 1mm).

In Figure TODO, the milled object is shown. As can be seen, the object is so far not transparent. The creases on the object are the result of the planar finishing. There also is a small surface that is very smooth and flat, this area was unfortunately not milled and is the raw acrylic glass. For more detailed discussion on artifacts and issues we refer to the section Discussion.
